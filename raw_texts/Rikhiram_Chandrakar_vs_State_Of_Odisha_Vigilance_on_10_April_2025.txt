15. Mr. Dhal, learned Senior Counsel for the petitioners submitted
that the central issue for determination in the present cases is whether
the exoneration of an accused in departmental proceedings, having
been concluded, criminal proceedings based on the same set of facts
should be allowed to continue or not. The learned counsel further
argued as to whether such proceedings should be deemed an abuse of
the process of the Court and a wastage of judicial time.

8. We are in respectful agreement with the above view. The purpose of departmental enquiry and of
prosecution are two different and distinct aspects. The criminal prosecution is launched for an
offence for violation of a duty, the offender owes to the society or for breach of which law has
provided that the offender shall make satisfaction to the public. So crime is an act of commission in
violation of law or of omission of public duty. The departmental enquiry is to maintain discipline in
the service and efficiency of public service. It would, therefore, be expedient that the disciplinary
proceedings are conducted and completed as expeditiously as possible. It is not, therefore, desirable
to lay down any guidelines as inflexible rules in which the departmental proceedings may or may not
be stayed pending trial in criminal case against the delinquent officer. Each case requires to be
considered in the backdrop of its own facts and circumstances. There would be no bar to proceed
simultaneously with departmental enquiry and trial of a criminal case unless the charge in the
criminal trial is of grave nature involving complicated questions of fact and law. Offence generally
implies infringement of public (sic duty), as distinguished from mere private rights punishable
under criminal law. When trial for criminal offence is conducted it should be in accordance with

statutory rules or law. That the strict standard of proof or applicability of the Evidence Act stands
excluded is a settled legal position. The enquiry in the departmental proceedings relates to the
conduct of the delinquent officer and proof in that behalf is not as high as in an offence in criminal
charge. It is seen that invariably the departmental enquiry has to be conducted expeditiously so as to

nature of evidence in criminal trial is entirely different from the departmental proceedings. In the

25. It is pertinent to note that the judgment in Radheshyam Kejriwal (supra) pertains to exoneration
in adjudication proceedings, whereas the issue in the present cases concerning the interplay
between departmental proceedings and subsequent criminal proceedings. Additionally, while the
judgment in Ajay Tyagi (supra) specifically addresses departmental proceedings, the subsequent
decision in Ashoo Surendranath (supra) did not rely on the ratio of Ajay Tyagi (supra) (a three-judge
bench decision) but instead referred to the rulings in P.S. Rajya (supra) (a two-judge bench
decision) and Radheshyam Kejriwal (supra), which may not be directly relevant to the issue under
consideration.
26. The rival submission of the learned counsels appearing for the parties prompt this Court to delve
upon the distinguishable elements between the departmental and adjudicative proceedings.
The key difference lies on the very nature of proceeding, departmental proceeding aims to the
disciplinary purpose of a delinquent officer on the basis of service rules and regulations which is an
administrative action whereas adjudication proceeding by its nature involves adjudication by a
Quasi-Judicial Authority. It determines the guilt of the persons facing the proceeding culminating
into penalty order. Therefore, the departmental proceeding deals with service related misconduct
within an organization while adjudication proceedings involve a legal dispute resolution process
conducted by an independent authority under statutory law. The law is no longer res integra that
"there is distinction between the procedure, purpose and finding of adjudication proceeding and
departmental proceeding." Adjudication proceedings are conducted by statutory bodies under the
Act, tribunals, or quasi-judicial authorities, adhering to procedures akin to a formal trial. In
contrast, departmental proceedings are inquiries conducted by departmental authorities, distinct in
their purpose and methodology from adjudicatory processes.

Since the departmental proceeding deals with internal disciplinary action rather than criminal
liability, the threshold for proof is lower and must establish that misconduct is more likely than not
to have occurred, purely the preponderance of probability. However, in the adjudication
proceedings clear and convincing evidence must be brought on record which should be stronger
than "Preponderance of Probability", but not as stricter as "Beyond Reasonable Doubt".
27. Therefore the attempt of the learned Senior Counsel for the petitioners to draw parallel between
the departmental proceeding with adjudication proceeding is misconceived. Hence, exoneration in
the departmental proceeding which is the culmination of much lower standard of prove cannot
enure to the advantage of the petitioners which would lead to quashing of the criminal proceeding.

this Court has already settled the dispute and this Court is bound by the judgement in Dr.
Minaketan (supra). He rather suggested for referring the matter to a bench of larger combination, in
case this Court is not agreeing with the view of Dr. Minaketan (supra).
29. I have carefully evaluated the judgment in Dr. Minaketan (supra), in which the coordinate Bench
of this Court has only borrowed the reasoning of Radheshyam Kejriwal (supra) and quashed the
proceedings. Moreover, in Dr. Minaketan (supra) the exoneration in departmental proceeding had
undergone judicial scrutiny by the Orissa Administrative Tribunal (OAT) and then by this Court in
W.P.(C) No. 2225 of 2015 which was dismissed 19.12.2017. Again an S.L.P. was filed before Hon'ble
Supreme Court being SLP (Civil) No.23691 of 2018, which came to be dismissed on 19.07.2019.
Therefore the coordinate Bench of this Court by adopting the reasoning of the decision of
Radheshyam (supra) granted benefit of departmental exoneration to the delinquent officer and
quash criminal prosecution qua him. Hence, the judgment of the coordinate Bench of this Court in
Dr. Minaketan (supra) is clearly distinguishable and does not lay down subtle ratio as propounded
by the petitioners herewith.
30. It is further noteworthy to mention that in all the cases under consideration, notwithstanding
the exoneration of petitioners in departmental proceedings, the competent authority on evaluation
of the materials on record accorded sanction to prosecute the petitioners. After grant of sanction by
the competent authority, the effect of exoneration in departmental proceedings loses its sanctity
because the sanctioning authority accords sanction to prosecute the accused on the material placed
before him which form the part of the charge sheet and arrived at the satisfaction that prima facie
materials are there against the public servant which warrant prosecution.
31. It is relevant to note that the present set of cases involved the allegation of P.C. Act. The P.C. Act
is special statute and this Act is enacted keeping in view to curtail the rampant corruption by the
public servants. The corruption in our country not only possesses a grave danger to the constitution
of governance, it is also threatens the very foundation of Indian democracy under Rule of Law. The
magnitude of corruption coming to the liability has all potential to choke the development of the
nation and undermines the Rule of Law. Therefore, it is the duty of the Court to interpret the
corruption law to strengthen the fight against corruption. In R.S. Nayak v. A.R. Antulay14, the
Constitution Bench of the Hon'ble Supreme Court has held that:

conclusion:-
(1984) 2 SCC 183
a) The standard of proof in the departmental proceedings being preponderance of
probability cannot be equated with the standard of proof required in a criminal
prosecution.
b) The exoneration of the petitioners in the departmental proceedings shall not enure
to the benefit of public servant who is facing charges of PC Act, 1988, which would
lead to quashing of the entire criminal prosecution.
c) The adjudication proceeding and departmental proceeding are distinct, the former
involves adjudication whereas the latter is not an adjudication.
d) The benefit of the judgment of Radheshyam (supra) cannot be extended to the
petitioners as the same judgment deals with adjudication proceeding, but not in
house departmental proceedings.
e) The judgment of the coordinate Bench of this Court in Dr. Minaketan (supra) is
factually distinguishable from the cases in hand.
f) Once the Sanctioning Authority accords sanction, the exoneration of the public
servant in the departmental proceeding loses its effect. Hence, dropping of the
criminal prosecution on exoneration in departmental proceeding is not tenable.
g) The judgment of the Hon'ble Supreme Court in Ajay Tyagi (supra) passed in 2012
by three Judge Bench supersedes the ratio laid down by the Division Bench of
Supreme Court in PS Rajya (supra) in 1996.
h) The exoneration of accused in a departmental proceeding shall have limited
bearing in regard to the offences under special statute however, diminish the effect
once the sanction for prosecution under special statutes is granted.
33. Therefore, in light of the aforementioned discussion and judgements cited, this Court is of the
considered view that there exists a fundamental distinction between adjudication proceedings and
departmental proceedings. Moreover, given that the judgment in Ashoo Surendranath (supra)
